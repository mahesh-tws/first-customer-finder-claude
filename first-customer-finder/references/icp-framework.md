# ICP Framework

Build this before researching anything -- it's the filter every prospect gets
checked against, so a sloppy ICP produces a sloppy shortlist no matter how
much research follows.

## Primary ICP

Answer, specifically:

- **Who** has this problem acutely -- role/title, team size, company size,
  industry, geography if relevant?
- **What triggers the pain** -- what has to be true about their situation for
  the problem to actually hurt (a threshold crossed, a manual process
  breaking down, a compliance deadline, a tool being sunset)?
- **Why now** -- what would make this person plausibly act in the near term
  rather than "someday"?
- **Who can plausibly adopt or buy** -- do they have the authority, budget,
  or influence to move on this themselves, or would they need to convince
  someone else first?

Avoid ICPs defined only by firmographics ("Series A B2B SaaS companies").
Firmographics narrow the search; they don't tell you who's actually hurting
right now. Pair them with a behavioral trigger.

## Adjacent ICP

A second profile worth including if the primary ICP doesn't produce enough
strong prospects in the target mode. Usually one of:
- A different role at the same kind of company (e.g. primary is the
  practitioner, adjacent is their manager)
- A different company profile with the same underlying pain
- A slightly earlier or later stage of the same journey (e.g. primary is
  "already evaluating alternatives," adjacent is "hasn't started looking yet
  but showing the same pain signals")

Keep primary and adjacent prospects clearly labeled and separated in the
final report -- don't blend them into one undifferentiated list.

## Disqualifiers

List the traits that make someone a bad fit despite looking similar on the
surface. Common categories:
- **Wrong size** -- too small to have the problem yet, or too large that this
  category of tool wouldn't clear their procurement bar
- **No authority** -- publicly enthusiastic but not in a position to adopt or
  influence adoption
- **Problem already solved** -- they've publicly mentioned a solution already
  in place that fully addresses this
- **Wrong geography/regulatory environment** -- if the product has
  jurisdiction-specific constraints
- **Signal too old** -- a pain point mentioned long enough ago that it may no
  longer be current (flag a staleness cutoff appropriate to the domain --
  a fast-moving tooling complaint from 18 months ago is weaker evidence than
  one from last month)

State disqualifiers explicitly so a prospect can be excluded on principle
rather than vibes, and so the user can see what got filtered out and why if
they disagree.
