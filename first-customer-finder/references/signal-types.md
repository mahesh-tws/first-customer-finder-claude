# Signal Types and Where to Look

Five categories of public signal. A strong prospect usually has evidence in
at least one; the highest-confidence prospects often stack two or more.

## 1. Demand signals

Someone publicly asking for a solution: "does anyone know a tool for X,"
"looking for recommendations on Y," a feature-request thread, a public RFP or
tender.

**Where:** community forums (Reddit, Hacker News, niche Slack/Discord
communities that have public archives, Stack Exchange), GitHub
issues/discussions on adjacent open-source projects, public RFP/tender
listings for B2B and public-sector targets, "looking for alternatives to
[competitor]" threads.

## 2. Pain signals

Public complaints about the current way of doing things -- a negative
review, a rant thread, a support-ticket-turned-public-post, a conference
talk describing a struggle.

**Where:** review sites (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius-style pages for adjacent
or competing tools -- read the 2-3 star reviews, they're the richest source),
public post-mortems and engineering blog posts, conference talk
titles/abstracts and recordings, public social posts.

## 3. Workaround signals

Someone describing a manual, duct-taped process standing in for a real
solution -- spreadsheets, custom scripts, someone's part-time job being "the
thing this product would automate."

**Where:** engineering/product blog posts describing internal tooling,
conference talks about "how we built our own X," public GitHub repos that
are clearly internal workaround tools, forum posts describing a manual
process in detail.

## 4. Switching signals

Evidence someone is migrating away from a competing or adjacent tool, or
explicitly comparing alternatives.

**Where:** "migrating from X to Y" blog posts, public changelog/deprecation
announcements from a competitor (their users become your prospects),
comparison threads, churn-adjacent public complaints ("we're finally moving
off of X").

## 5. Timing signals

Something that makes now a plausible moment to act, even without explicit
pain language.

**Where:** funding announcements (a raise often triggers new tooling
budgets), job postings for a role tied to the problem (a hiring push signals
the team is scaling into the pain point), a recent product launch or public
roadmap item that creates a new need, a recent regulatory or compliance
deadline relevant to the ICP, a recent public event (acquisition,
leadership change, expansion into a new market).

## Search strategy by mode

- **quick / design-partners** -- a handful of targeted searches per signal
  type most likely to surface people who'd give feedback (demand + workaround
  signals tend to be richest here)
- **standard** -- broad coverage across all five categories, moderate depth per category
- **deep** -- broad coverage plus deliberate cross-referencing -- look for the
  same pain pattern showing up across multiple independent sources, which is
  itself evidence worth calling out in the report
- **b2b** -- weight timing and switching signals heavily; individuals matter
  less than company-level triggers
- **community** -- weight demand signals heavily; prioritize explicit public
  asks over inferred pain

## What doesn't count as a signal

- A company merely existing in the target industry, with no public statement
  tying them to the problem
- A LinkedIn title match with no public post or activity beyond the profile
  itself
- Anything requiring a login, a paid data/enrichment service, or scraping a
  platform's private API to obtain
